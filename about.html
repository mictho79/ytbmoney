Why the "industry-standard" calculators get it wrong for Shorts

Most online calculators are built around long-form CPM math (typically $5–$15 CPM × 40% monetized × 55% revenue share). That formula is fine for long-form. It is completely wrong for Shorts, because:

  1. Most Shorts views aren't monetized. YouTube calls these "swipe-past" impressions — they count as views but never trigger an ad. Only intentional views (sustained engagement) are in the revenue pool.
  2. Shorts revenue is a pool, not per-impression CPM. YouTube allocates the Shorts Fund across all creators based on engagement share. The math isn't "CPM × views × split" — it's share-of-attention divided by pool size.
  3. Audience geography destroys the "global average" assumption. Which brings us to the next screenshot.

Where my viewers actually come from

Look at that list. India, Brazil, Algeria, Turkey, France. Four of the top five are Tier-2 or Tier-3 advertising markets where RPMs run between $0.01 and $2 per 1,000 views. France is the single Tier-1 country in the top 5 — and it's only 4.8% of views.

When a generic calculator tells a sports/entertainment creator "you'll earn $3,000 on 1M views at $3 RPM," it's silently assuming a mostly US/UK/German audience. No sports Shorts channel has a mostly US/UK/German audience. This mismatch is why creators keep feeling ripped off by YouTube — they weren't. The calculator they used was wrong.

Audience demographics matter too

For football/Shorts content, the audience is 64.6% male and ~60% aged 25–44. That's a very different advertiser profile than beauty (female-skewed, premium cosmetics CPM), or finance (older, affluent, insurance/broker advertisers). The niche you're in directly shapes which advertisers bid on your inventory — and that shapes your RPM. The best-niches guide walks through why finance and home-improvement clear $10–$20 RPM while sports and gaming clear $1–$3.

How this calculator's numbers are sourced

Every RPM figure in this tool is cross-checked against:

  • My own YouTube Studio dashboard (48.4M views / $1,283 over 28 days, screenshots above).
  • Public creator disclosures — creators who post their earnings on Twitter/X, Reddit r/NewTubers, or their own videos.
  • Tubular Labs and Social Blade estimates, used as sanity-check benchmarks (not as primary source — their numbers are often dated).
  • Community data from monetized creator Discord servers and Shorts Fund beta participants.

Numbers are updated when the evidence shifts — not on a marketing calendar. The last full RPM review was April 2026.

Why I stay anonymous

I don't link my channel here, and that's deliberate. Two reasons:

  1. Advertiser cross-reference risk. If a brand can tie my channel's analytics to this site's traffic, it gives them leverage in sponsorship negotiations I'd rather not hand out.
  2. Competitor playbook. Football Shorts is a copycat-heavy niche. Publishing the exact channel makes it trivial for someone to scrape my whole content strategy.

What this site will publish is real numbers, real screenshots, and real methodology. The channel identity is the only thing held back.
