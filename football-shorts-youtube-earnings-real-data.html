Why football Shorts RPM is structurally low

I get this question from other creators constantly: "Your views are massive — why isn't your RPM higher?" The answer is four overlapping factors, in order of weight:

1. Audience geography — the biggest single driver

Look at that screenshot. Four of the top five countries are Tier-2 or Tier-3 advertising markets. India CPMs run $0.50–$1.50 for sports inventory; Brazil runs $1–$2; Algeria and Turkey are even lower. These numbers are the raw auction prices advertisers pay YouTube — before YouTube's 45% cut. My effective RPM is whatever's left after that cut, applied to a geography-weighted blend of those CPMs.

If you want the math: a weighted-average CPM of ~$1.20 × 40% monetized rate × 55% creator share = $0.26 RPM on long-form content with my audience. On Shorts it drops another 3-5× because of the pool model (more on this below), landing around $0.05 RPM intentional. For the full country breakdown, see YouTube RPM by country.

Contrast with NFL channels, where 75-80% of views come from the United States. Same content format (sports highlights), same platform, same Shorts mechanics — but 10× the RPM because the advertiser bids are 10× higher.

2. The Shorts pool model vs CPM auctions

Long-form YouTube revenue is simple: advertiser bids $X for 1,000 impressions, YouTube takes 45%, you keep the rest. Every impression has its own price.

Shorts revenue is different. YouTube takes all Shorts ad revenue globally each month, subtracts music licensing costs, and distributes the remaining pool across creators based on their share of total intentional views. Your individual Short doesn't have a CPM — it has a share of a pool.

The practical effect: even if your next viewer is a US finance executive, that view isn't worth more than a view from an Indonesian teenager — because the pool already happened, and you're just earning a slightly larger slice of it. The geography shapes which countries contribute to the pool, not what your individual view is worth.

This is counterintuitive for creators coming from the long-form world, and it's the primary reason Shorts RPM feels "broken" compared to long-form expectations.

3. Audience demographics and advertiser fit

My audience is 64.6% male, 35.2% female, and ~60% aged 25-44. That is an advertiser profile, and it's not a particularly expensive one. Sports betting, gaming gear, energy drinks, and low-ticket fashion dominate my ad fill. Compare this to:

  • Beauty channels — 80%+ female, Sephora/L'Oréal/Estée Lauder all bidding → higher CPM
  • Finance channels — older, affluent, Credit cards/brokerages/insurance bidding → much higher CPM
  • B2B tech channels — decision-makers 30-55, SaaS companies bidding → premium CPM

For the full comparison of audience profiles by niche, see Best YouTube Niches for High RPM. The short version: sports content attracts advertisers, but not premium advertisers.

4. Copyright and Content ID friction

Football content is some of the most heavily monitored on YouTube. Premier League, UEFA, FIFA, La Liga, Serie A, Bundesliga — every one of them has Content ID fingerprints for every match broadcast. Any Short using match footage either gets instantly claimed (revenue flows to the rights holder, not me) or gets the ad load reduced.

My channel sidesteps this by using almost exclusively compilation footage from cameras, stadium angles, and post-match interviews that aren't in the Content ID database. But even then, a small percentage of my Shorts get partial claims. This further reduces effective RPM by probably 5-10%.

Five things I learned that aren't in any YouTube advice thread

1. Shorts volume beats Shorts quality — up to a ceiling

For the first 30,000 subscribers, uploading more Shorts (3-4/day vs 1/day) moved my revenue almost linearly. Past 30k subs, quality started mattering more than quantity — the algorithm began filtering low-retention Shorts out of the Recommended feed aggressively, so bulk-publishing mediocre content actively hurt my reach. Current sweet spot is 2 high-effort Shorts per day.

2. Most "Shorts RPM tips" online are myths

Longer Shorts don't pay more per view. Adding music doesn't pay more per view. Shorts Fund ended in 2023 — every current RPM is just ad pool share. The only levers that actually moved my RPM were: (a) shifting audience geography toward Tier-1 where possible, and (b) avoiding demonetized content categories (violence, politics).

3. The January Q1 dip is real and brutal

In the last week of December 2025, my daily revenue pace hit ~$40/day. In the first week of January 2026, it collapsed to ~$15/day. Same views, same content, 62% revenue drop. Q1 seasonality is not a rounding error for Shorts — it's a cliff. Plan cash flow accordingly.

4. Sponsorships at 50K subs pay 10-30× AdSense per hour of work

I took my first sponsorship deal at 32k subs. A single Short with a 15-second sponsor integration paid $220 for maybe 2 hours of editing. That is higher than an entire week of AdSense on my non-sponsored Shorts. This is where Shorts-only creators under 200k subs should focus if they want income, not raw subs.

5. The "views go up, RPM goes down" rule

When one of my Shorts goes viral (2M+ views), it almost always lands in Tier-3 countries first (India, Pakistan, Indonesia). My RPM drops during viral weeks because the view mix shifts down-tier. If you see your RPM dropping while your views are growing, this is almost certainly why — not a YouTube bug, not demonetization, just geography rebalancing under your feet.

Frequently asked questions about this data

How much does a 50K subscriber football Shorts channel earn?

A 50,000-subscriber football Shorts channel earns approximately $500–$1,500 per month from YouTube AdSense alone, depending on view volume and audience geography. This page documents $1,283.55 earned over 28 days on 48.4 million views — roughly $0.05 RPM per 1,000 intentional Shorts views.

What is a typical RPM for football Shorts in 2026?

Typical RPM for football Shorts ranges from $0.03 to $0.08 per 1,000 intentional views. The real channel documented on this page averages $0.05. This is 30-100× lower than long-form football content, which clears $0.80–$3 RPM.

Why is your RPM so different from what YouTube money calculators predict?

Most online calculators use long-form CPM math ($3 RPM × views ÷ 1,000) which is structurally wrong for Shorts. Shorts use a revenue pool model, Shorts views are mostly "swipe-past" non-monetized views, and my audience geography is ~95% Tier-2/Tier-3 — all three factors compound to produce the 50× gap between predicted and actual.

Can a football Shorts channel scale to a full-time income?

From AdSense alone, most football Shorts channels under 500K subscribers cannot clear a full-time living wage in a Tier-1 cost-of-living context. To clear $5,000/month from AdSense at my RPM, I would need roughly 100 million monthly views. Sustainable full-time income comes from layering sponsorships, betting affiliates, merchandise, and digital products on top of AdSense.

Do you factor in sponsorship revenue in these numbers?

No. The $1,283.55 / 28 days figure is AdSense only — the YouTube Studio "Estimated revenue" metric. Sponsorship revenue is tracked separately and runs roughly 2-3× the AdSense number on months with active deals. This page focuses on AdSense because that is what calculators attempt to predict.

How did you get YouTube Partner Program approved at 1,000 subs?

The 2023 YPP threshold update lowered the requirement to 500 subscribers + 3,000 watch hours (OR 3 million Shorts views in 90 days) for limited monetization, and 1,000 subs + 4,000 watch hours for full monetization. I hit the full tier in November 2025. See YouTube Partner Program requirements for the current rules.

Why don't you link your actual channel?

Two reasons: (1) Advertisers I negotiate sponsorships with shouldn't be able to cross-reference my public traffic stats with this site's traffic — that's a lever I don't hand over freely. (2) Football Shorts is an aggressive copycat niche where publishing exact channel identifiers makes my whole editorial strategy cloneable overnight. The screenshots above are the proof of authenticity; the channel name is not necessary.
