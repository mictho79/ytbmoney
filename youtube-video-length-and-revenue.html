YouTube Video Length and Revenue: Does Longer = More Money?

Longer videos can earn more — but only up to a point. Here's where the sweet spot really is for most creators.

The 8-Minute Mid-Roll Threshold: Why It Changes Everything

YouTube introduced the mid-roll ad policy requiring videos to be at least 8 minutes long to qualify for ads placed in the middle of a video. This single rule reshaped how creators approach video length — and for good reason. Mid-roll ads can increase a video's total ad revenue by 20–40% compared to a video of equal views with only pre-roll ads.

The logic is simple: a viewer who is 5 minutes into your video is far more invested than one who just clicked play. They're less likely to abandon the video when an ad appears, which means advertisers pay more for mid-roll placements, driving up your effective RPM.

Video Length vs. Revenue: A Comparison by Length Bucket

Not all video lengths perform equally. The table below summarizes the trade-offs across common video lengths, assuming average engagement rates for a general-audience channel.

Video Length Mid-rolls Enabled Typical RPM Boost Best Strategy
Under 8 min No Baseline (0%) Maximize views; rely on CTR and CPM of pre-rolls
8–10 min Yes (1 mid-roll) +15–25% Efficient for tight topics; 1 mid-roll at natural break
10–15 min Yes (1–2 mid-rolls) +25–40% Sweet spot for most niches — best RPM without heavy drop-off
15–20 min Yes (2–3 mid-rolls) +35–50% Works well for tutorials, deep-dives, and documentary content
20+ min Yes (3+ mid-rolls) +40–60% (potential) High risk of completion rate drop; only recommended for loyal audiences

Watch Time Decay: The Hidden Cost of Going Long

Here is the counterintuitive part: longer videos don't automatically earn proportionally more money. Every video has a retention curve — a graph showing what percentage of viewers are still watching at each point in the video. The longer your video, the steeper the average drop-off.

A 20-minute video may technically qualify for 3 mid-roll ads, but if only 30% of viewers make it to the second mid-roll placement at the 13-minute mark, you're generating far fewer impressions from that mid-roll than the ad slot count implies. YouTube's algorithm also factors average view duration into search and recommendation rankings — a video that people finish tends to rank higher than one with high drop-off.

Key finding: The 10–15 minute range is the sweet spot for most YouTube niches. It clears the mid-roll threshold comfortably, allows 1–2 well-placed mid-roll ads, and keeps average completion rates high enough to satisfy the algorithm and advertiser demand simultaneously.

Optimal Length Strategy by Niche

The "right" length varies significantly by content type. Here's what performs best by category:

  • Finance & business tutorials: 12–18 minutes. Audiences expect depth and tolerate longer formats. RPM is naturally high.
  • Tech reviews: 10–14 minutes. Enough time for a full product walkthrough with comparison segments.
  • Gaming: 10–20 minutes. Gameplay content benefits from longer watch sessions, but aim for clear structure.
  • Beauty & lifestyle: 8–12 minutes. Audiences are engaged but trend toward tighter edits.
  • Cooking: 8–12 minutes. Recipe walkthroughs naturally fit this window.
  • Educational/explainer: 10–15 minutes. Enough time to cover a topic thoroughly without losing attention.
  • Vlogs & entertainment: 12–20 minutes. Storytelling formats reward longer views when pacing is strong.

The Bottom Line: Length Is a Tool, Not a Target

Padding a video to hit 10 minutes with filler content is one of the fastest ways to tank your audience retention and long-term channel performance. YouTube's algorithm rewards engaged watch time, not raw minutes. The best strategy is to make every minute earn its place — then check that your video clears the mid-roll threshold. If your content naturally lands at 9:45, adding 30 seconds of value to cross 10:00 is worthwhile. Adding 2 minutes of padding is not.
